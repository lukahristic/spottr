# Skill: Spottr Voice Calibration

Use this skill **in addition to** `spottr-copy.md` and `spottr-principles.md`. Those define the foundational warmth. This file calibrates the strength of that warmth so it doesn't drift into soft, precious, or therapeutic territory.

---

## The recalibration

Spottr's voice is **warm, not soft.** The existing copy guide leans hard into reassurance — which is right — but reassurance without confidence reads as fragility. Users need to trust that Spottr knows what it's doing.

The corrected voice profile:

- **Quietly confident** — not loud, not apologetic
- **Clear** — specific beats poetic
- **Intentional** — every choice signals brand
- **Trustworthy** — earned via specifics
- **Modern** — reads 2026, not 2018
- **Social but not needy**
- **Warm when appropriate**
- **Strong when needed**

What it must **never** become:
- Corporate
- Overly therapeutic
- Patronizing
- Earnest in a way that sounds performative
- Quasi-poetic ("a familiar face" used as decoration)

---

## The five tone checks

Before publishing any user-facing copy, run it past these:

1. **Specific or vague?** Specific wins. "12 people here right now" beats "Some friends nearby."
2. **Apologetic?** If it sounds like Spottr is asking permission, rewrite. Spottr is allowed to be there.
3. **Could this appear in a meditation app?** If yes, rewrite.
4. **Does it patronize?** Users know what a gym is. Don't over-explain emotional dynamics.
5. **Does it sound like a real person who knows what they're saying?** Or does it sound like a brand performing thoughtfulness?

## Side-by-side calibration

The existing copy guide has good examples but some lean too soft. Stronger alternatives:

| Current direction | Calibrated direction |
|---|---|
| "Take your time." | "Take your time." (keep — this is exactly right) |
| "You're all set." | "You're checked in." (specific > vague affirmation) |
| "A familiar face can change a workout." | Cut. Too poetic. Doesn't say anything concrete. |
| "Some days start with a nod." | Cut. Same reason. |
| "Quiet right now. That happens." | "Quiet right now. Invite someone." (turns the empty state into an action) |
| "You're not training alone." | "You're not training alone." (keep — works as a headline because the product earns it) |
| "Intro sent. They'll see it." | "Intro sent." (cut the explanation; the user knows what intro means) |
| "Hmm, that didn't work. Try again." | "That didn't work. Try again." (drop the "hmm" — it's a tic, not warmth) |

## Empty states — the most important copy in the product

The existing guide says empty states should be warm and reassuring. That's necessary but insufficient. Empty states are the moment users decide if Spottr is alive or dead. They must be **honest + actionable.**

| Surface | Old style | Calibrated |
|---|---|---|
| Live tab, no one checked in | "Quiet right now. That happens." | "You're the only one here right now. Invite someone you know at this gym." (with button) |
| Inbox, no threads | "No conversations yet." | "No conversations yet. Open up to someone on the live tab." |
| Profile, no gyms added | "Add your first gym." | "Add a gym to start checking in." (with button) |
| Live tab, only the user's gyms list is empty | "Add a gym to see who's around." | Keep — this is fine |

Pattern: every empty state must have a clear next action. "Quiet right now" alone is a wall. "Quiet right now. Invite someone." is a door.

## Confidence vs. softness — the line

| Too soft | Calibrated |
|---|---|
| "We hope you find what you're looking for." | (delete entire sentence; never say this) |
| "We're here if you need us." | "If something's off, email safety@spottr.app." |
| "Your safety matters." | "Here's exactly what we do to keep you safe." (then show, don't tell) |
| "Take all the time you need." | "Take your time." (shorter is stronger) |
| "We just want to make sure..." | "To check you in, we use GPS once. Then it's done." (concrete) |

Softness is a tic of products that don't trust themselves. Spottr does. The voice should reflect that.

## CTAs — specifically

CTAs are where weak voice shows up most.

Good (already in copy guide):
- Check In
- I'm Ready
- Send Intro
- Reply
- Done

Calibrated additions:
- Add this gym (not "Add gym")
- Invite to [Gym Name] (gym-specific, not generic)
- Block (not "Block User" — the noun is implied)
- Report (same)
- Open to chat (toggle label — "I'm open to chat" is weaker)

Avoid:
- "Tap to continue" (tap is the default — don't narrate the interface)
- "Get started" (vague)
- "Learn more" (lazy)

## Push notifications — the highest-stakes copy

A push notification has 30 characters of attention. Every word fights for itself.

| Event | Good | Better |
|---|---|---|
| Someone replied to your intro | "You got a reply" | "Princess replied." |
| 3 people just checked in at your gym | "Activity at your gym" | "3 people just checked in at Anytime BGC." |
| You've been blocked from a thread | (don't notify — silent) | (don't notify — silent) |
| Your gym just went live on Spottr | "Anytime BGC is on Spottr" | "Anytime BGC is on Spottr. You're the first 50." |

Pattern: names and specifics > categories and abstractions.

## What to do when you're stuck on tone

If a piece of copy isn't working, the answer is usually one of these:

1. **Make it shorter.** The shorter the line, the more confident it reads.
2. **Make it specific.** Replace adjectives with numbers, names, or facts.
3. **Cut the apology.** Anything that softens the message is usually weakening it.
4. **Remove the rhetorical move.** "Some days start with a nod" — cut.
5. **Ask: would a person say this out loud?** If no, rewrite until yes.

---

## When in doubt, run the audit

Before any major copy ship:

- Open the screen / page / email
- Read every line aloud
- Mark anything that sounds like a brand performing rather than a person speaking
- Rewrite those lines

This audit takes 10 minutes and catches 80% of voice drift.
